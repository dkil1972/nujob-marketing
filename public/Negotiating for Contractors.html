Negotiating for Contractors

Get that rate increase on your contract extension. Get a better deal on your new projects and leave that 'market rate' behind.

You want to charge a higher rates, but you don't know where to start. You negotiated yourself the 'market rate' and tell yourself that your client can't pay higher for you.

When a contract extension comes up, rather than negotiating yourself a better deal, you take the easy option - you accept the same terms; vowing to negotiate on the 'next one'.

Most of us have spent years perfecting our technical skills. We know how to deliver a great user experience, a killer app, or solid code. Most of us haven't even spent a single hour learning how to negotiate.

What if you could get a better rate on your next contract?

As a contractor, we find ourselves in a negotiation situation fairly regularly. Whether it is an extension or a brand new contract, there is a deal to be had approximately every 6 months. What would a higher rate mean? It's worth investing in negotiation skills simply because of the opportunity to benefit from it regularly.

Some of us know we are bad at negotiating and hate getting into the money discussion at all - we're glad when it's all over and we can get on with the job. This course aims to give you the tools to mak it go smoothly but still get a desirable outcome.

Others think they are great at negotiating, some of possibly are! But do they know exactly what they are doing? or could they have got an even better deal? This course will show you what you are doing right and what you are doing wrong.

What took me 3 years and $1,000,000+ in lost revenue to figure out:

I didn't want to "push my luck". I felt that if I charged too much, I'd lose my clients... and I'd be out of work.

Having worked with thousands of freelancers over the last few years, I've realized this is the #1 reason why freelancers are underpaid, struggle with cashflow, and end up more stressed than they ever were back in the cubicle. It's all due to fear and self-doubt. That feeling of: "I don't want to screw this up."

As demand for my work grew, I started to build a team. Before I knew it, I had 11 full time employees working for me. This gave me a ton of exposure to a lot of clients and their projects.

I started to really dig into why we were winning some projects and losing others. I began talking to clients and fellow agency owners to figure out why some people were, in my mind, "getting away with" charging significantly higher than their competition.

Here's what I walked away with:

  • Good clients don't always go for the lowest price.
  • The price you quote is factored into your "risk profile", and if you can reduce risk through other means you can raise your prices without losing clients.
  • How you position yourself and your services is everything. (Hint: Don't call yourself a freelancer!)
  • Successful freelance consultants never negotiate on price.
  • Cheap overseas labor isn't going to put us out of business, but it can if you're a commodity.

I was able to raise my rates (and my team's rate) from $50 an hour to over $20,000 a week. And there's no reason you can't also.

Once you're able to understand why people buy you'll be able to reposition your services and price in a way that is best for you AND your client. Here's the thing: No one has ever hired you for your design, development, marketing, writing, etc. skills. We often forget that clients don't really care about that, and we often make the mistake of selling that — instead of what clients really need from us.

Clients want to understand how you're going to be an investment. They want to make sure that if they pay you that they make more than they're paying.

This means you need to understand the problem behind each project. I like to call this the "proxy pain". What's really making someone willing to take out their checkbook and pay you?
